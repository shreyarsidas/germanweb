About

Two Languages, One Conversation

English and German share a common Germanic ancestor — Proto-Germanic — spoken over 2,000 years ago. Despite diverging dramatically, the two languages never stopped borrowing from each other.

During the 19th and 20th centuries, German philosophy, science, and psychology flooded English: Weltanschauung, Kindergarten, Angst, Schadenfreude, Wanderlust. Meanwhile, post-WWII American cultural dominance sent waves of English words back into German: Computer, Selfie, Baby, Hobby, Hamburger.

Today, this exchange accelerates with the internet, pop culture, and global commerce — making both languages richer and more expressive.

Geschichte · History

A Timeline of Mutual Influence

~500 BCE – 400 CE
Common Germanic Roots
Both English and German descend from Proto-Germanic. Words like Wasser / water, Brot / bread, and gut / good reveal their shared ancestry — these aren't loanwords, they're twins.
9th – 15th Century
Medieval Trade & the Hanseatic League
German merchant guilds dominated North Sea and Baltic trade. English absorbed terms like huckster (from Low German hökster) and trade words that shaped the commercial vocabulary of early English.
17th – 18th Century
Science, Mining & Music
German miners and scientists introduced cobalt, nickel, quartz, zinc, feldspar into English scientific vocabulary. German musical terminology — Leitmotiv, Walzer (waltz), Lied — followed the spread of German classical music across Europe.
19th Century
Philosophy, Psychology & Kindergartens
The golden age of German intellectual exports. Angst, Weltanschauung, Zeitgeist, Kindergarten, Gesundheit entered English through German philosophy, Freudian psychology, and the influence of German immigrant communities in America.
1945 – 1980
The American Wave: Denglisch Begins
Post-WWII American cultural dominance flooded German with English terms. Baby, Baby-sitter, Hobby, Teenager, Job, Computer arrived wholesale, often with adapted spelling. Critics coined "Denglisch" — the contested mix of Deutsch and English.
1990s – Present
Digital Culture & Continuous Exchange
The internet accelerated borrowing in both directions. German adopted Selfie, Hashtag, Fake News, Shitstorm (the latter now in German dictionaries). English, in turn, embraced Schadenfreude, Wanderlust, Doppelgänger, Wunderkind as concepts with no clean English equivalent.
